Sacrifice £20,000 into pension to escape the £100k taper trap
£12,000 /yr

Your income of £120,000 is in the £100k–£125,140 Personal Allowance taper band where your effective marginal rate is 60% (40% income tax + 20% from the lost £1-per-£2 PA reduction). Salary-sacrificing £20,000 into your pension would bring your taxable income to £100,000, restoring your full Personal Allowance and saving approximately £12,000 in tax this year. The sacrificed amount also accrues in your pension for retirement.

Action steps
  1. Confirm employer offers salary sacrifice (most do)
  2. Request £20,000 sacrifice — typically arranged for next payroll
  3. Confirm employer NIC saving (13.8% / 15%) is rebated to you (some employers split this)
Information only — salary sacrifice has irreversible consequences. Speak to a qualified financial adviser before acting.

Phase your £212,500 tax-free cash over 10 years instead of lump sum
£2,696 /yr

Taking your full £212,500 25% tax-free cash in one year forces the remaining 75% (£637,500) into drawdown and likely additional-rate tax bands. Instead, crystallise £21,250 per year for 10 years. Each year you take £5,313 tax-free + £15,938 taxable. The taxable portion stays within basic-rate band where possible. Estimated tax saving vs lump-sum approach: ~£26,960 over the period, with the additional benefit that uncrystallised SIPP balance continues to grow tax-free.

Action steps

  2. Set up annual crystallisation of £21,250
  3. Coordinate with state pension start (age 66/67) to avoid stacking
  4. Review yearly — adjust if rules change or circumstances shift
Information only. Pension crystallisation is irreversible — speak to a qualified pension specialist before acting.

Bed-and-ISA: move £12,000 from GIA to ISA before April 5
£216 /yr

You hold £380,000 in your General Investment Account (GIA), exposed to income tax on dividends and CGT on gains. Your ISA has £12,000 unused allowance this year. Sell £12,000 from your GIA — using your £3,000 CGT Annual Exempt Amount to minimise CGT — and immediately re-buy the same holdings inside your ISA. Future dividends and gains on this portion are then tax-free permanently. ISA allowances cannot be carried forward, so missing this deadline loses the wrapping opportunity for these specific funds.

Action steps
  1. Calculate which GIA holdings have lowest unrealised gains (minimise CGT)
  2. Sell £12,000 worth of these GIA holdings
  3. Immediately re-buy the same holdings inside your ISA (most platforms automate this as "Bed and ISA")
  4. Complete before April 5
Information only. Bed-and-ISA timing matters — consult your platform and adviser.
ISAR 1998 Reg 4 (annual limit) + TCGA 1992 s.3 (CGT AEA)
Pre-April-2027 SIPP strategy: 320 days to plan
£340,000 lifetime

From 6 April 2027, defined contribution pensions (including SIPPs) enter the estate for Inheritance Tax. Your £850,000 SIPP currently sits outside the estate. From April 2027, this exposes up to £340,000 to 40% IHT on death (subject to nil-rate bands). Strategies to consider: (a) accelerate phased crystallisation pre-2027 to spend SIPP first; (b) gift surplus drawdown income to remove from estate (s.21 IHTA exemption for regular gifts out of normal expenditure); (c) review nominations to favour adult children with own pension allowance. The April 2027 effective date is fixed — Royal Assent received 18 March 2026.

Action steps
  1. Decide: spend SIPP first (favours pre-2027) vs preserve SIPP for beneficiaries (favours pre-75 death)
  2. Review beneficiary nominations for tax efficiency
  3. Project IHT exposure under both scenarios with adviser
  4. Re-check April 2027 — possible scheme to delay further (legislation still evolving)
Information only. SIPP and IHT planning is high-stakes — speak to a Chartered Tax Adviser AND pension specialist together.

To avoid the 30-day "bed-and-breakfast" rule, replace the sold holding with a similar-but-not-identical fund (e.g. swap a Vanguard FTSE 100 tracker for an iShares FTSE 100 tracker).

Information only. Loss harvesting is subject to anti-avoidance rules — speak to a Tax Adviser.
